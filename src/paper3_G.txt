WHAT WE WON'T DO (too expensive, diminishing returns):
Full QM energy surfaces for every residue in your 2.5M-row dataset — obviously impossible. But more specifically:

Ab initio MD on full proteins. Even a single 100-residue protein at DFT level is ~10⁵ CPU-hours per nanosecond. You'd need thousands of structures. Dead on arrival.
CCSD(T) benchmarks for every (φ,ψ) bin. The gold-standard QM method, but a single alanine dipeptide scan at CCSD(T)/CBS is a week of compute. Doing this for 20 amino acids × multiple χ₁ states × environment contexts is years of work. Groups like Head-Gordon and Sherrill have spent careers on this.
QM-derived partial charges per residue per conformation. RESP charges depend on the electronic environment, so in principle every (φ,ψ,χ₁) state has different charges. Computing this for your full dataset would require millions of QM calculations. Not tractable, and the payoff is marginal — AMBER's fixed charges work reasonably well for the electrostatic torques you already compute.
Dispersion-corrected DFT for all LJ pairs. Your current LJ uses classical Lennard-Jones with AMBER parameters. QM dispersion (DFT-D3/D4) would give more accurate van der Waals interactions, but the improvement over well-parameterized LJ is typically ~0.1–0.3 kcal/mol per pair. Not worth recomputing for 2.5M residues.


WHAT OVERLAPS WITH PAPERS 1 & 2 (QM validates or reinterprets your findings):

The n→π interaction.* This is the big one you already flagged but deferred. The carbonyl lone pair (C=O of residue i) delocalizes into the π* orbital of the next carbonyl (C=O of residue i+1). This is purely quantum mechanical — no classical force field captures it. It stabilizes αR and PPII conformations by ~0.5–1.0 kcal/mol per residue, and it's strongly φ/ψ-dependent. Your Paper 1 steric discontinuity at φ ≈ −60° and the αR basin being a "geometric intersection" — the n→π* interaction is part of why αR is special. It's the electronic glue that makes the steric intersection stable.
Peptide bond partial double character. Your Paper 2 finding that ω deformation couples to local packing — the classical explanation is "steric strain rotates the peptide plane." The QM explanation is that ω ≈ 180° is maintained by π-electron delocalization across N–C(=O), and deviations from planarity cost delocalization energy. The two explanations make different predictions about which perturbations are most effective at distorting ω.
Hyperconjugation in β-branched residues. Your Paper 2 finding that VAL/ILE/THR drive the largest Cβ angle variations — classically you attribute this to steric bulk. But β-branched residues also have σ(Cβ–Cγ) → σ*(Cα–N) hyperconjugative interactions that are conformationally dependent. This is why β-branching affects backbone geometry more than expected from sterics alone.
Charge redistribution with conformation. Your electrostatic torques use fixed AMBER charges. In reality, the partial charge on the backbone N changes by ~0.05e between αR and β conformations (due to different orbital overlap). This is small but systematic, and it means your electrostatic torque channel has a hidden conformation-dependent bias that classical mechanics can't capture.

SAPT decomposition for key H-bond geometries (~1–2 weeks). Paper 1 (Mechanical Equilibrium) asks: why is a residue where it is? It reframes the Ramachandran plot from a population map into a force field. Each (φ,ψ) position is the deterministic outcome of a local mechanical balance — steric torques dominate, they transmit across the Cα junction, and the three secondary structure types emerge as three distinct mechanical regimes. The αR basin isn't held by restoring forces; it's trapped by two orthogonal steric walls whose intersection happens to be (−60°, −40°). This is the causal foundation.Paper 2 (Multi-Scale) asks: given that a residue is at (φ,ψ), what happens to the bond angles? It shows that the mechanical structure established in Paper 1 propagates further — from dihedrals into three-dimensional geometry through the Cα center. Sidechain class sets the amplitude of distortion, χ₁ sets the direction, and the environment adds a third, weaker non-local correction. Bond angles are not fixed parameters; they are outputs of a mechanical hierarchy.Paper 3 (Coupling) asks: can backbone energetics be treated as separable one-dimensional terms? The answer is formally no. Using ANOVA it quantifies exactly how much variance the φ×ψ interaction term contributes to each observable — 15% to 51% depending on angle. It also shows that electronic corrections (n→π*) are geometrically redundant, and maps the energetic magnitude of coupling to ~1.6 kcal/mol, comparable to a hydrogen bond.
