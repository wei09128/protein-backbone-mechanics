------3, partial double bond peptide plane deformation------
The peptide plane is a partial double bond. It resists deformation. So when we see τ deforming at residue i, something must be pushing on it. What's pushing?
Local backbone coupling — φ(i) and ψ(i) themselves rotate the peptide plane, so τ(i) flexes because the two neighbouring peptide planes are tugging it through the Cα
2. Sidechain lever — the Cβ sits off the peptide plane. χ1 rotation + sidechain mass creates a torque that pushes through the Cα and twists τ
3. Non-local / through-space — a neighbouring residue's atoms (H-bond partner, steric clash 5 Å away) apply a force that pulls Cα sideways, deforming τ

τ = ∠N-Cα-C. The Cα has four substituents in a tetrahedron: N, C, Cβ, Hα. The angle τ between N-Cα and Cα-C is the one we measure. If Cα were a perfect sp³ atom with no strain, τ would be 109.47° always. It's not. In real proteins τ ranges from ~103° to ~120°.

What τ actually is, geometrically
τ = ∠N-Cα-C. The Cα has four substituents in a tetrahedron: N, C, Cβ, Hα. The angle τ between N-Cα and Cα-C is the one we measure. If Cα were a perfect sp³ atom with no strain, τ would be 109.47° always. It's not. In real proteins τ ranges from ~103° to ~120°.
The classical view says τ flexes because the sp³ hybridisation is soft — the force constant is ~80 kcal/mol/rad², stiff but not infinite. Your paper 1 view says τ flexes because the mechanical force field pushes it. Both views are true, but the mechanical view tells you what's doing the pushing.
The subtlety you're pointing at — "through what?"
You asked three things and they're all distinct:

Through Cβ (sidechain): The Cβ is rigidly attached to Cα by a single bond with its own stiffness. Any force on the sidechain (sidechain packing, solvent, H-bond to a sidechain atom) propagates to Cα through that bond, which then deforms τ through the sp³ geometry constraint. This is the "sidechain lever" channel.
Through backbone atoms (N, C, O, H): Forces on N (incoming H-bond as acceptor), O (outgoing H-bond as donor acceptor), H (amide H donor), or the neighbouring N/C through peptide bonds — all propagate into Cα through the N-Cα and Cα-C bonds. This is the "backbone strain" channel. It's direct and should dominate.
Through-space non-bonded interactions: Steric clashes with atoms 3-5 Å away, electrostatic pulls from charged sidechains of distant residues. These don't go through any bond — they act directly on Cα's position. This is the "through-space" channel.

The beautiful thing: each channel leaves a different fingerprint on the τ deformation, and you can separate them statistically. That's paper 2's real contribution.
